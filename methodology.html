Methodology

A four-phase filter used to move from raw data to the conclusions presented in the Thesis section. The goal is to separate stated justifications for an outcome from the structural conditions that produced it, and to avoid treating correlation as proof.

Phase 1

Input Filter

A question is only analyzed if it can be reduced to a measurable mechanism — hardware constraints, mathematics, or a falsifiable structural relationship. Questions that depend on unverifiable claims about individual or collective intent are set aside, because there is no operational data that can confirm or disconfirm them.

Rejected: "Is policy X morally good?" — not falsifiable.
Accepted: "What happens to household debt-to-income when the policy rate is held below the inflation rate for an extended period?" — measurable and falsifiable.
Phase 2

System Boundary Mapping

The system under examination is isolated from its background environment. Inputs, processing mechanisms, outputs, and feedback loops are mapped explicitly, and a falsifiability condition is stated in advance: a specific empirical result that would disprove the working hypothesis. If no such condition can be named, the analysis is treated as incomplete rather than presented as conclusion.

Phase 3

Structural Causality Parsing

Stated justifications for a policy or outcome are set aside in favour of the underlying constraints that produced it — fiscal architecture, demographic structure, resource limits. Where possible, the relationship between variables is mapped as a directed path (A mechanically affects B) rather than asserted as a general association.

Phase 4

Probabilistic Simulation

Complex systems are non-linear, so outcomes are treated as probabilities conditioned on the available data, not certainties. A baseline expectation is established from historical data, the relevant structural change is introduced, and the resulting trajectory is described in terms of likelihood rather than inevitability.

Why this approach?

Most public commentary on national decline relies on anecdote or partisan framing. This methodology is an attempt to constrain the analysis to what the data actually supports: documented metrics, multi-decade time series, and structural relationships that can be checked against the source tables in the Data section. Conclusions presented in the Thesis section are intended to be revisable — if new data contradicts a stated finding, the finding changes. Every such correction is logged in the Changelog.

Metric Selection

The 51 metrics tracked in the Data section were chosen because they are standard indicators used by national statistical agencies and international bodies (Statistics Canada, OECD, IMF, Bank of Canada) to assess a country's economic and social health — not because they were expected to show any particular trend. That most currently point toward stress rather than improvement is a finding produced by applying this methodology to the data, not a criterion used to select the data.

Reference framework

Karl Popper — falsifiability as the test for whether a claim counts as analysis rather than narrative.

Judea Pearl — distinguishing correlation from structural, mechanical causality.

Thomas Bayes — updating the probability of an outcome as new data becomes available, rather than treating any single data point as final.

This page describes method, not conclusions. For the findings produced by applying this framework to Canadian economic and demographic data, see the Thesis section. For the underlying source data, see the Data section.
